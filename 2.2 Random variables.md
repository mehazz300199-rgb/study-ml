# 2.2.1 Discrete Random Variables

**Definition**: A random variable $X$ is **discrete** if its sample space X is **finite or countably infinite**.

**Probability Mass Function (PMF)** The PMF computes the probability of the random variable taking a specific value $x$:

$$p(x) \triangleq Pr(X = x)$$

**Core Properties**:
1. **Range**: $0 \leq p(x) \leq 1$
2. **Normalization**: $\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) = 1$

**Common Examples**:
- **Uniform Distribution**: All $K$ possible outcomes are equally likely ($p(x) = 1/K$).
- **Degenerate (Delta) Distribution**: All probability mass is on a single value $k$, represented by the indicator function $p(x) = \mathbb{I}(x = k)$

# 2.2.2 Continuous Random Variables

$X$ is called a **continuous random variable** when $X \in \mathbb{R}$ represents a real-valued quantity. Unlike discrete variables, a finite or countable set of distinct values cannot be created for continuous variables; instead, probability is associated with the variable falling into intervals on the real line.

## 2.2.2.1 Cumulative distribution function (CDF)

The **CDF** of a random variable $X$ is defined as the probability that $X$ is less than or equal to a specific value $x$:
$$P(x) \triangleq Pr(X \leq x)$$

**CDF** are characterized as monotonically non-decreasing functions. The probability that $X$ falls within a finite interval $(a, b]$ is calculated as the difference between the CDF evaluated at the two endpoints:
$$Pr(a < X \leq b) = P(b) - P(a)$$

## 2.2.2.2 Probability Density Function (PDF)

The **PDF** is defined as the derivative of the CDF:
$$p(x) \triangleq \frac{d}{dx} P(x)$$
*Note that this derivative does not always exist, in which case the pdf is not defined*

The probability of a continuous variable being in a finite interval is the integral of the pdf over that range:
$$Pr(a < X \leq b) = \int_{a}^{b} p(x) dx = P(b) - P(a)$$

For an extremely small interval $dx$, the probability of $X$ falling within that range is the density at $x$ multiplied by the width of the interval: $Pr(x < X \leq x + dx) \approx p(x) dx$

## 2.2.2.3 Quantiles

If the **CDF** $P$ is strictly monotonically increasing, it has an inverse function $P^{-1}(q)$, known as the **inverse CDF**, **quantile function**, or **percent point function** (PPF).

If $P$ is the **CDF** of $X$, then $P^{-1}(q)$ = $x_q$ mean that $\operatorname{Pr}(X \leq x_q) = q$;.

*Note: Quantile is a function with input is a point show how much probability have accumulated up to that point: $\operatorname{Pr}(X \leq x_q)$*

**Some definitions**:
- $P^{-1}(q) = x_q$ $then$ $\operatorname{Pr}(X \leq x_q) = q$.
this is called the $q$'th **quantile** of $P$.
- The value $P^{-1}(0.5)$ is the **median** of the distribution, with half of the probability mass on the left, and half on the right.
- The values $P^{-1}(0.25)$ and $P^{-1}(0.75)$ are the lower and upper **quartiles**.

**Example**: $\Phi$ is CDF, and $\Phi^{-1}$ is the quantile function (inverse cdf). Then $\Phi^{-1}(x)$ = $a$ mean that the probability that we select a number $\leq a$ is $x$

# 2.2.3 Sets of related random variables

## **Joint Distributions**
The joint distribution of two random variables, denoted $p(x, y)$, represents the probability that $X = x$ and $Y = y$ occur simultaneously. For variables with finite cardinality, this can be represented as a 2D table where every entry sums to one.

## **Marginal Distributions (The Sum Rule)**
The marginal distribution of a single random variable is found by summing the joint distribution over all possible states of the other variable(s):

$$p(X = x) = \sum_{y} p(X = x, Y = y)$$

This is also known as the sum rule or the rule of total probability.
   
## **Conditional Distributions (The Product Rule)**
The conditional distribution of a variable given the value of another is defined as:

$$p(Y = y | X = x) = \frac{p(X = x, Y = y)}{p(X = x)}$$

$$p(X = x, Y = y) = {p(Y = y | X = x)}\cdot{p(X = x)} = {p(X = x | Y = y)}\cdot{p(Y = y)}$$

Rearranging this formula gives the product rule, which is used to calculate the joint probability: $p(x, y) = p(x)p(y|x)$.
   
## **The Chain Rule**:
By extending the product rule to $D$ variables, we arrive at the chain rule of probability:

$$p(x_{1:D}) = p(x_1)p(x_2|x_1)p(x_3|x_1, x_2) \dots p(x_D|x_{1:D-1})$$

This rule provides a foundational method for constructing high-dimensional joint distributions from a set of simpler conditional distributions.

## **Notes**

- *If two variables are independent, their joint distribution is simply the product of their marginals: If $X$ and $Y$ are independent then $p(x, y) = p(x) . p(y)$*.

- *The distribution and marginal distribution of a single random variable $X$ are technically the same but when talking about marginal distribtion of $X$ we are saying calculate the probability of $X$ using another random variable $Y$*.

# 2.2.4 Independence and Conditional Independence

## Unconditionally independent or Marginally independent

Two random variables $X$ and $Y$ are considered unconditionally independent or marginally independent, denoted by $X \perp Y$, if their joint distribution can be represented as the product of their individual marginal distributions:
$$p(X, Y) = p(X)p(Y)$$

*Unfortunately, unconditional independence is rare, because most variables can influence most other
variables. However, usually this influence is mediated via other variables rather than being direct.*

## Mutually independent

A set of variables $X_1,\dots,X_n$ is (mutually) **independent** if the joint can be written as a product of marginals for all subsets $\{X_1,\dots,X_m\} \subseteq \{X_1,\dots,X_n\}$:
$$p(X_1,\dots,X_m) = \prod_{i=1}^m p(X_i) \tag{2.22}$$

*Example*: $X_1, X_2, X_3$ are mutually independent if the following conditions hold:

$p(X_1, X_2, X_3) = p(X_1)p(X_2)p(X_3)$, $p(X_1, X_2) = p(X_1)p(X_2)$, $p(X_2, X_3) = p(X_2)p(X_3)$, and $p(X_1, X_3) = p(X_1)p(X_3).^2$

## Conditionally independent

$X$ and $Y$ are conditionally independent (CI) given $Z$ if the conditional joint
can be written as a product of conditional marginals:
$$X \perp Y \mid Z \iff p(X, Y \mid Z) = p(X \mid Z)p(Y \mid Z)$$

# 2.2.5 Moments of a distribution

## 2.2.5.1 Mean (expected value) of a distribution

### Definition

Mean is the expected value of the distribution denoted by $\mu$ defined as follow:

- For continuous random variable (If the integral is not finite, the mean is not defined):
$$\mathbb{E}[X] \triangleq \int_{\mathcal{X}} x \, p(x) dx \tag{2.24}$$
- For discrete random variable (this only helpful if the random variable is ordered in some way):
$$\mathbb{E}[X] \triangleq \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} x \, p(x) \tag{2.25}$$

### Properties

- Linear operator:
$$\mathbb{E}[aX + b] = a\mathbb{E}[X] + b \tag{2.26}$$

- For a set of n random variables, one can show that the expectation of their sum is as follows:
$$\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] \tag{2.27}$$

- If the random variables are **independent**, the expectation of their product is given by:
$$\mathbb{E}\left[\prod_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] \tag{2.28}$$

## 2.2.5.2 Variance of a distribution

### Definition

The **variance** is a measure of the "spread" of a distribution, often denoted by $\sigma^2$. This is defined as follows:

$$\mathbb{V}[X] \triangleq \mathbb{E}\left[(X - \mu)^2\right] = \int (x - \mu)^2 p(x)dx \tag{2.29}$$
$$\quad = \int x^2 p(x)dx + \mu^2 \int p(x)dx - 2\mu \int xp(x)dx = \mathbb{E}\left[X^2\right] - \mu^2 \tag{2.30}$$

We got:
$$\mathbb{E}\left[X^2\right] = \sigma^2 + \mu^2 \tag{2.31}$$

The **standard deviation** is defined as:
$$\text{std}[X] \triangleq \sqrt{\mathbb{V}[X]} = \sigma \tag{2.32}$$

### Properties

- Linear operator:
$$\mathbb{V}[aX + b] = a^2\mathbb{V}[X] \tag{2.33}$$

- If we have a set of n **independent** random variables, the variance of their sum is given by the sum of their variances:
$$\mathbb{V}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{V}[X_i] \tag{2.34}$$

- The variance of the **independent** random variables product can also be derived, as follows:
$$\mathbb{V}\left[\prod_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\prod_i X_i\right)^2\right] - \left(\mathbb{E}\left[\prod_i X_i\right]\right)^2 \tag{2.35}$$
$$\quad = \mathbb{E}\left[\prod_i X_i^2\right] - \left(\prod_i \mathbb{E}[X_i]\right)^2 \tag{2.36}$$
$$\quad = \prod_i \mathbb{E}\left[X_i^2\right] - \prod_i (\mathbb{E}[X_i])^2 \tag{2.37}$$
$$\quad = \prod_i \left(\mathbb{V}[X_i] + (\mathbb{E}[X_i])^2\right) - \prod_i (\mathbb{E}[X_i])^2 \tag{2.38}$$
$$\quad = \prod_i (\sigma_i^2 + \mu_i^2) - \prod_i \mu_i^2 \tag{2.39}$$

## 2.2.5.3 Mode of a distribution

The **mode** of a distribution is the value with the highest probability mass or probability density:
$$\boldsymbol{x}^* = \underset{\boldsymbol{x}}{\operatorname{argmax}} \, p(\boldsymbol{x}) \tag{2.40}$$

## 2.2.5.4 Conditional moments

**Law of iterated** expectations, also called the **Law of total expectation**:
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}_Y [\mathbb{E}[X|Y]] \tag{2.41}$$
*Note*: $\mathbb{E}[X|Y=y]$ is expected value of $X$ given $Y = y$.

*Proof*:
$$\mathbb{E}_Y [\mathbb{E}[X|Y]] = \mathbb{E}_Y \left[ \sum_x x \, p(X = x|Y) \right] \tag{2.42}$$

$$\quad = \sum_y \left[ \sum_x x \, p(X = x|Y = y) \right] p(Y = y) = \sum_{x,y} x p(X = x, Y = y) = \mathbb{E}[X] \tag{2.43}$$

**Law of total variance**, also called the **Conditional variance formula**:
$$\mathbb{V}[X] = \mathbb{E}\left[X^2\right] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = \mathbb{E}_Y \left[s_{X|Y}\right] - \left(\mathbb{E}_Y \left[\mu_{X|Y}\right]\right)^2 \tag{2.46}$$

$$\quad = \mathbb{E}_Y \left[\sigma_{X|Y}^2\right] + \mathbb{E}_Y \left[\mu_{X|Y}^2\right] - \left(\mathbb{E}_Y \left[\mu_{X|Y}\right]\right)^2 \tag{2.47}$$

$$\quad = \mathbb{E}_Y [\mathbb{V}[X|Y]] + \mathbb{V}_Y [\mu_{X|Y}] \tag{2.48}$$

with:
$$\mu_{X|Y} = \mathbb{E}[X|Y]$$
$$s_{X|Y} = \mathbb{E}\left[X^2|Y\right]$$
$$\sigma_{X|Y}^2 = \mathbb{V}[X|Y] = s_{X|Y} - \mu_{X|Y}^2$$
